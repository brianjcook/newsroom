# The Wareham Times Style Guide (Practical Reference)

## Overview
This document summarizes key editorial, grammatical, and stylistic conventions for The Wareham Times, based on some aspects of the NY Times style guide. It is suitable for newsroom writing, long-form journalism, and high-quality digital publishing.

---

## 1. Tone and Voice

- Mostly formal, but accessible and just the slightest bit folksy
- Clear, precise, restrained
- Avoid slang, clichés, and unnecessary jargon
- Narrative allowed, but always grounded in verified facts
- Neutral and non-partisan in news reporting

---

## 2. Names and Attribution

### First Reference
- Use full name: `John A. Smith`

### Subsequent References
- Use honorific + last name:
  - `Mr. Smith`, `Ms. Smith`, `Dr. Smith`

### Honorific Rules
- Use: Mr., Ms., Mrs., Dr.
- Avoid using first names after initial reference (except in features or informal contexts)

### Anonymous Sources
- Use sparingly
- Must include justification:
  - "said on condition of anonymity because..."

---

## 3. Grammar and Mechanics

### Serial (Oxford) Comma
- Always use:
  - `red, white, and blue`

### Numbers
- Spell out:
  - One through nine
- Use numerals:
  - 10 and above
- Always use numerals for:
  - Ages: `5 years old`
  - Money: `$5`, `$10 million`
  - Statistics: `3%`

### Dates
- Format:
  - `March 19, 2026`
- Include comma between day and year

### Titles
- Capitalize before name:
  - `President Biden`
- Lowercase after name:
  - `Joe Biden, the president`

---

## 4. Punctuation

### Quotation Marks
- Periods and commas go inside quotes:
  - `"This is correct," she said.`

### Dashes
- Use em dashes (—) for interruptions or emphasis
- Avoid excessive use

### Apostrophes
- Standard possessive rules apply:
  - `James’s book` (preferred)

---

## 5. Headlines

- Use sentence case:
  - `Mayor announces new housing plan`
- Avoid clickbait
- Be precise and factual
- SEO considerations allowed but restrained

---

## 6. Paragraph Structure

- Short paragraphs preferred (especially digital)
- Typically 1–3 sentences
- Use subheads for long articles

---

## 7. Style and Word Choice

### Preferred Characteristics
- Specific over vague
- Concrete over abstract
- Active voice preferred

### Avoid
- Redundancies:
  - ❌ "past history"
- Inflated language:
  - ❌ "utilize" → ✅ "use"

---

## 8. Inclusive Language

- Use gender-neutral language when possible
- Avoid assumptions about identity
- Use person-first language when appropriate:
  - `person with a disability`
- Follow current standards for race/ethnicity terminology

---

## 9. Ethics and Editorial Standards

- Accuracy above all
- Verify all claims
- Attribute clearly
- Disclose conflicts of interest
- Maintain strict separation:
  - News vs Opinion

### Corrections
- Prompt, transparent corrections required

---

## 10. Formatting and Digital Conventions

### Links
- Use descriptive anchor text:
  - ❌ "click here"
  - ✅ "view the full report"

### Structure
- Use subheadings
- Use bullet points where appropriate
- Optimize for readability

---

## 11. Common NYT vs AP Differences

| Rule                | NYT                  | AP                  |
|---------------------|----------------------|----------------------|
| Oxford comma        | Yes                  | No                   |
| Honorifics          | Yes (Mr., Ms.)       | No                   |
| Tone                | Slightly narrative   | More concise         |
| Headlines           | Sentence case        | Often title case     |

---

- [ ] Use Oxford comma
- [ ] Include honorifics after first reference
- [ ] Spell out numbers 1–9
- [ ] Use sentence case for headlines
- [ ] Keep tone neutral and precise
- [ ] Avoid clichés and jargon
- [ ] Verify all facts and sources
- [ ] Use short, readable paragraphs
